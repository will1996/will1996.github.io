Cloth Parameter Estimation

Will Chambers Caroline Horsch

will1996@umd.edu chorsch@umd.edu

Goal: Reconstruct cloth from single-view, real-world photos

Given a sequence of images of a piece of cloth, determine detailed information about the physical nature of the cloth, such that it can be virtually reconstructed and simulated in a physically accurate way.

Motivation:

Artist workflows

Modern movies and games have huge need for realistic materials for creatives. Physically accurate materials are intuitive to work with, and robust to many needs. However, they're difficult to sculpt to an artists intent because they have many (often dozens) of parameters to tweak. Automating this process gives better, automatic results, enabling artists to create, rather than perform data entry.


Virtual Try On

Online shopping fails to communicate the fit and feel of clothing in particular. Addressing this shortcoming makes online shopping far more useful to the consumer


Sim To Real - Training

True to life simulations enable training people (or AI) to react to real world scenarios without putting anyone or any thing at risk. Cloth parameters can equally well apply to tissues, tendons. Simulating these materials accurately can provide safe, essential training environments for doctors and first responders.


Background

Differentiable Rendering

Differentiable renderers apply mathematical transformations to rendering operations to make the process of coloring screen pixels based on 3D data a differentiable operation. This means we can take derivatives of the rendering process with respect to 3D data parameters, thereby enabling gradient descent based optimization of rendering parameters, or "Inverse rendering"

Differentiable Physics

Differentiable physics simulators take special care with collision handling to allow derivatives to be taken of the simulation with respect to various parameters, including material parameters. This enables back-propagation of loss through the simulation processes, thereby allowing gradient descent based optimization of physical parameters.

Projective Dynamics

Projective Dynamics is a relatively new approach at modeling dynamics (proposed by Bouaziz, Sofien, et al. 2014 "Projective dynamics: Fusing constraint projections for fast simulation."), which poses the dynamics problem as a set of paralell energy minimization problems of quadratic energy potentials defined on non-linear constraints. The generality and simplicity of the method has made it popular among implementors and researchers alike, and it recieved two key improvements in recent years. (Ly, Mickaël, et al. 2020 "Projective dynamics with dry frictional contact.") Added efficient self-contact handling, enabling efficient cloth simulation under heavy self contanct, and (Li, Yifei, et al. 2022 "DiffCloth: Differentiable cloth simulation with dry frictional contact.") made Projective Dynamics efficiently differentiable for cloth.

State of the Art:

There are many approaches to virtual cloth reconstruction. While these works achieve good results, they do not work well on real-world cloth photos and rely on multi-view images or videos of cloth motion over time. Our method aims to address these drawbacks.

Learning Based:

In 2017, Liang et al applied LSTM/CNN techniques to this problem. They trained the CNN to track the folds in cloth under controlled conditions, then an LSTM to track these changes over time. The output of this network was one of 54 physical parameter classes for cloth determined to be significant.


Differentiable Cloth based:

In 2020, Liang et al applied a Differentiable Cloth simulation to a sequence of 3D meshes. Given this sequence their simulator was able to faithfully reconstruct the cloth parameters.


GradSim:

In 2021, Gradsim used a pipeline consisting of a differentiable renderer and a differentiable simulator to optimize cloth using image losses. While this method works very well for simulated environments, its renderer prevents it from working in the real world, and it requires video.

Our Approach:

Quasi-Static Simulation

The folding pattern of a piece of cloth will be different depending on the properties of the material. For example, a stiffer cloth will show less draping and folding than a less stiff cloth. In prior work, multi-view images or videos are necessary to recreate the cloth. However, by taking advantage of the relationship between quasi-static drape pattern and physical properties, we can identify the physical parameters from just a single image.

Renderer

(borrowed and modified)

Redner Differentiable Renderer

Offers many rendering parameters, but for our project, we only need to focus on mesh material parameters.

Available visual material parameters:

    • Shine, roughness, and color


We have access the gradient of a loss with respect to these parameters or with respect to other parameters like vertex position, camera pose, etc. for a rendered image.

Optimization - Gradient Derivation

In order to optimize all parameters via gradient descent, we need to derive an equation for the gradients of the image loss (produced by the renderer) propagated all the way back through the renderer and simulator to the physical parameters.

Provided by the renderer: Derivative of the image loss function with respect to the vertex positions of the mesh.

Provided by the simulator: Derivative of total sum of forces on the mesh with respect to the physical parameters, k.

We need to derive an equation for derivative of the image loss with respect to the physical parameters, using the values for these derivatives which we have

Data Generation

In order to evaluate our system, we need a dataset of cloth images which follow the fixed, standardized quasi-static draping setup. Using real world photos would require obtaining fabric samples, taking physical parameter measurements of each sample, and setting up the lighting, camera pose, and constraints in an extremely consistent way.

Synthetic data, which we can create directly using our system, is easier to obtain and easier to solve, making it a good starting point for testing and evaluation. Before creating a dataset of real world photos, we will generate simulated data and use it to evaluate the method.

Our Process for Generating Synthetic Data Samples

In the simulator:

  1. Pick random pairs of values for the stretch stiffness and bending stiffness

  2. hang a 16x16 mesh with these properties held by a single vertex and apply gravity force

  3. When cloth has settled, save the mesh for rendering

In the renderer:

  1. Pick random values for shine and roughness

  2. Load the simulated mesh and apply a material with the selected parameters

  3. Render and save image of the cloth

Final Result:

A set of parameter values (stretch, bend, shine, roughness) and a corresponding image of cloth with these properties hanging quasi-statically.

Summary Of Contributions:


  • Modified Diffcloth to support parameter gradients of position, added this functionality to pytorch-autodiff enabling backpropagation

  • Modified Diffcloth to support quasi-static simulation

  • Updated Redner to function on up-to-date hardware with new ray-tracing backends and py-torch interfaces, correcting blocking stability issues

  • Designed and implemented optimization pipeline for parameter estimation from single images

Alternatives and Future Work:


Parameter Initialization via Classification

Previously, we have built a classifier which uses inverse rendering and spectral methods to identify the type of fabric seen in a photo. We will use this method to find a reasonable initial guess at the cloth parameters which will improve efficiency and accuracy of the reconstruction.

Sequential frames of video

Integrate the differentiable renderer into a setup similar to Liang et al 2017, with an LSTM attached. Use this configuration to understand the properties of the cloth from its motion, as well as physical parameters. Classify to a physical parameter set, and then refine with differentiable cloth physics.

Neural Material Representation

Rather than rely on existing simulation/rendering models, create a neural representation which encodes both sets of properties, and train this representation on both physical, and optical properties of cloth.
